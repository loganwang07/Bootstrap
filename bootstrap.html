  1. The Set-Up
    • the premise on which the joke is told
    • sets up the listener to anticipate something
    • can be an unusual fact, an incident (made up or real), or an observation
  2. Detail
    • add your details and escalate your story
    • should be a crisp, one-line statement
    • details can be fluid but must be immediately relevant
  3. Punchline
    • reverses the expectations of the set-up OR escalates it to an extreme

Puns


A pun, also known as paronomasia, is a form of word play that exploits multiple meanings of a term, or of similar-sounding words, for an intended humorous or rhetorical effect (Wikipedia definition). Puns have a long history in human writing. For example, the Roman playwright Plautus was famous for his puns and word games.

Fun Fact: Shakespeare used over 3,000 puns in his plays. Surprisingly, Elizabethan literature considered puns and wordplay to be a "sign of literary refinement" more so than humor.
  • What do you call a mean crook going down stairs? A condescending con, descending.
  • I had a dream I was swimming in a pool of soda. Too bad it was just a Fanta sea.
  • What did the grape say when he got stepped on? Nothing, he just let out a little wine.
  • Why could the mathematician only work from home? Because he could only function in his domain.
  • Did you know that I'm really good at bowling? It's right up my alley.
  • What happens when a parsley farmer gets sued? They garnish his wages.
  • How does Moses make his coffee? Hebrews it.
  • How was the Roman Empire split in two? With a pair of Caesars.
  • Did you hear about the cheese factory that caught fire? There was nothing left but de brie.

Books Never Written

Sometimes called "books never written" or "world's greatest books", these are jokes which consist of fictitious book titles with authors' names that contain a pun relating to the title. Perhaps the best-known example is: "Tragedy on the Cliff by Eileen Dover", which according to one source was devised by humourist Peter DeVries (1910-1993).

Fun Fact: Did you know that the Tanakh (the first five books of the Bible) contains puns?

Anti-jokes


An anti-joke is a type of humor in which the joke-teller says something that is intentionally unfunny. It usually relies on the audience's expectation of something humorous, and the irony of it not being funny is what provides the comedic value.

Fun Fact: A prank form of an anti-joke, known as no soap radio was popularized in 1950s New York.
